Addendum · March 2026

What's Changed Since January

The original thesis is intact. One new variable has made it urgent.

When this thesis was first written in January 2026, the core argument was structural: UPS was executing a deliberate volume mix shift — shedding unprofitable Amazon tonnage and redeploying capacity into healthcare logistics and SMB parcels — and the market was reading it as decline. That argument hasn't changed.

Q4 2025 confirmed the trajectory. UPS reported adjusted EPS of $2.38, beating consensus of $2.20, on revenue of $24.5B against a $24.0B estimate. Full-year 2025 came in at $88.7B in revenue with a 9.8% adjusted operating margin. Management reiterated 2026 guidance of $89.7B revenue and 9.6% adjusted operating margin — essentially flat revenue as Amazon volumes continue exiting, offset by mix improvement. The Network Reconfiguration program is now targeting $3 billion in savings for full-year 2026.

Labor, the fear that dominated headlines in 2023 and 2024, is behaving. The Teamsters contract was front-loaded; union wage growth slowed to approximately 3% by Q4 2024 and is not accelerating. SMB penetration reached 31.2% of U.S. volume in full-year 2025, up meaningfully year-over-year. Healthcare revenue grew to $11.2B.

What has changed is oil. In early January 2026, WTI was at roughly $78.50 per barrel — elevated but manageable. Since early March, a geopolitical crisis centered on the Strait of Hormuz has sent WTI above $98 at peak and Brent briefly to $112. As of late March 2026, WTI trades near $92 and Brent near $106. Goldman Sachs estimates a $15–20/bbl risk premium embedded in current prices — a premium over fundamentals, not a fundamental shift in supply. The market has reacted by selling UPS from ~$103 in January to ~$98 today. It is layering a transient energy shock onto an already-discounted transformation story. Both discounts, we believe, are wrong.

The Labor Overhang Is Backward-Looking

The Teamsters contract ratified in August 2023 was structured to deliver its largest wage increases in years one and two. That front-loading was real — and it compressed margins in 2023 and 2024. But by Q4 2024, union wage growth had slowed to approximately 3% year-over-year, a rate consistent with pre-pandemic labor cost management. Consensus models that project structural labor cost acceleration from here are reading a backward-looking fear into a forward-looking earnings stream. The contract runs through 2028. The step-up is behind us.

Pillar II · March 2026 Update

The Oil Overhang

The Hormuz crisis has created the most elevated fuel environment since 2022 — layered onto a stock already discounted for transformation risk.

On March 20, 2026, Iraq declared force majeure on a portion of its export volumes. Iran has rejected direct U.S. peace talks. The Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 21% of global oil supply transits daily — faces its most acute disruption threat in years. WTI hit $98.32 intraday; Brent briefly touched $112. Goldman Sachs, which had raised its 2026 Brent forecast to $85 per barrel before the spike (from $77), now estimates its March 2026 price at $110 and quantifies the geopolitical risk premium at $15–20 per barrel above supply-and-demand fundamentals.

UPS is acutely sensitive to oil. The market knows this and is acting accordingly: UPS has declined from roughly $103 in early January to roughly $98 today. The question is whether the market is right to treat the current oil price as a permanent input cost — or whether it is, as Goldman explicitly describes it, "a risk premium over fundamentals" that will compress as the geopolitical situation resolves.

We believe the latter. The thesis is not that oil will crash. It is that the $15–20 geopolitical premium is more likely to fade than persist, and that even a partial reversal toward pre-crisis fundamentals ($78–85 WTI) recovers meaningful margin while UPS continues executing its transformation. If both normalize simultaneously — oil and the transformation discount — UPS could see a re-rating on two dimensions at once.

Goldman Sachs describes the current pricing as "a risk premium over fundamentals." J.P. Morgan, while acknowledging that even a ceasefire may leave a lasting "security tax," still expects normalization toward lower supply-demand clearing prices as the geopolitical situation evolves.

Here is the core of the double re-rating thesis: if oil reverts toward $80, UPS recovers approximately 100bps of adjusted operating margin from the current ~$90 environment. On $89.7B in revenue, that is roughly $900M of operating income the market is currently not giving UPS credit for. Simultaneously, the stock is trading at 9.1× LTM EV/EBITDA against a five-year average of 11.6×. If transformation execution continues and energy costs normalize, the earnings power the market is pricing today is wrong on two dimensions at once. That is the setup.

Risk Assessment

Key Risks

Four specific risks that would invalidate the thesis or reduce the margin of safety.

Risk 01

Amazon Exit Exposes Real Share Loss

The bull case requires that enterprise shippers, SMBs, and healthcare customers absorb the volume UPS is deliberately shedding. If fill rates disappoint — if the capacity freed by the Amazon reduction does not attract higher-margin replacements — then revenue per piece improvements will be overwhelmed by volume declines. The "Better, Not Bigger" narrative becomes structurally negative rather than strategically transitional. Watch ADV trends in H2 2026 closely.

Risk 02

Healthcare & SMB Ramp Slower Than Modeled

Healthcare revenue was $11.2B in 2025. The $20B long-term aspiration requires sustained double-digit growth across a complex, relationship-driven vertical. Regulatory changes, cold-chain execution failures, or slower pharmaceutical volume growth could delay the ramp by multiple years. Similarly, SMB penetration gains require ongoing sales capacity investment. Any plateau at current levels undermines the pricing power thesis.

Risk 03

Oil Stays Elevated — The Security Tax Persists

The thesis depends on the Hormuz geopolitical risk premium fading. If it doesn't — if Iran-related escalation continues, or if broader regional conflict locks in a structural "security tax" on energy markets — the $15–20/bbl premium becomes permanent. Every quarter at $100+ WTI represents approximately 150bps of operating margin the model does not recover. This is the single most important near-term risk to the thesis.

Risk 04

Dividend Sustainability & Payout Ratio

UPS pays $6.56 per share annually — a payout ratio of approximately 100% of 2025 GAAP EPS. The 6.7% yield is a primary reason income investors own the stock. If margins do not inflect in 2026 and earnings do not grow, the Board faces a choice: cut the dividend or maintain a structurally unsustainable payout. A cut would trigger institutional forced selling and remove a key support layer for the stock. This risk is real and is not fully priced in.

Scenario Analysis

Bull & Bear

Bull Case $155 – $175
  1. Hormuz resolution + oil normalization. A ceasefire or diplomatic breakthrough sends WTI toward $75–80. UPS recovers 100–150bps of operating margin. The market re-rates on two dimensions simultaneously: earnings up, multiple expands. The double re-rating scenario plays out.
  2. Healthcare reaches $14–15B run rate. Continued cold-chain, hospital logistics, and pharmaceutical volume growth accelerates the segment well ahead of schedule, providing a visible organic growth engine the market can underwrite independently of the Amazon exit story.
  3. SMB crosses 33–35% of U.S. volume. Small business penetration continues to compound, driving revenue per piece improvement that more than offsets Amazon ADV declines. Q4 2026 shows clear year-over-year EBITDA growth for the first time since 2022.
  4. Network savings beat at $3.2–3.5B. Automation penetration reaches 70%+ by year-end; facility consolidations complete on schedule. Operating leverage begins to show clearly in quarterly results, restoring management credibility on the transformation timeline.
  5. Multiple re-rates toward historical average. As execution compounds, EV/EBITDA moves from 9.1× toward 10.5–11× — still below the 11.6× five-year mean. Dividend sustainability confirmed by Q2 2026 earnings. Institutional buyers return.
Bear Case $80 – $90
  1. Sustained $100+ WTI through 2026. The Hormuz situation escalates. A structural "security tax" takes hold. UPS absorbs ~200bps of ongoing margin drag that fuel surcharges only partially recover. The oil thesis inverts: energy costs become a permanent input cost headwind, not a transient premium.
  2. Amazon volumes not replaced. H2 2026 volume growth targets miss. Enterprise and SMB fill rates disappoint. Revenue declines while the cost structure adjusts more slowly, creating a margin squeeze that compounds the oil drag. The transformation narrative loses credibility.
  3. Healthcare ramp stalls below $12B. Execution risks in cold-chain logistics, competitive pressure from specialized players, or pharma volume softness cap segment growth. The $20B aspiration is pushed out by three or more years, removing the most compelling forward growth story.
  4. Dividend cut triggers institutional selling. The Board reduces the quarterly dividend from $1.64. Income-oriented holders are forced sellers. The yield re-sets at a lower absolute level while removing the income floor that has historically limited downside and supported the multiple.
  5. Macro slowdown reduces SMB demand. A U.S. economic slowdown hits SMB shipping volumes disproportionately. Small businesses ship less; B2B parcel volumes slow; the mix-shift thesis stalls as the target segments themselves contract. Revenue misses and multiple compression arrive simultaneously.
